This repository was generated by [PReSto](https://paleopresto.com) (Paleoclimate Reconstruction Storehouse) from the [Holocene DA Template](https://github.com/DaveEdge1/presto-holocene_da). It runs the offline paleoclimate data-assimilation method of [Erb et al. (2022)](https://doi.org/10.5194/cp-18-2599-2022) — implemented in the original [Holocene-Reconstruction/Holocene-code](https://github.com/Holocene-Reconstruction/Holocene-code) repository — to reconstruct spatially complete temperature fields over the parameters and proxy selection captured below.

## About this reconstruction — a bison body-size case study

North American bison lost a substantial fraction of their body size through the Late Quaternary, and the cause has been debated for decades. Proposed drivers include warming (Bergmann's rule), declining forage productivity and quality, population density, and human hunting, probably acting together and episodically (e.g. Hill et al. 2008). This custom Holocene reconstruction, generated for the PReSto Workshop (June 2026), tests one of those drivers, the thermal hypothesis, by giving each specimen the temperature of the place and time it actually lived.

**Why data assimilation.** Earlier thermal tests usually relied on a single hemispheric temperature series. Martin et al. (2018, *Ecology & Evolution*), whose body-size data I reuse here, related bison body mass to a Greenland ice-core (GISP2) record. A single global series varies only in time, so by itself it cannot separate a temperature effect from the passage of time, and it says nothing about the local climate each bison experienced. Observation-constrained, spatially-explicit data assimilation removes both limitations and lets the thermal hypothesis be tested with the best available temperature estimate.

**What this reconstruction is.** An observation-constrained, spatially-explicit annual-mean surface-temperature reconstruction for North America over 0–12,000 cal BP, produced by Holocene paleoclimate data assimilation (Temp12k proxies assimilated into a HadCM3 + TraCE-21ka model prior; 100-yr steps; 104-member ensemble). Full settings are in the Reconstruction parameters and Proxy data selection sections below.

**How I used it.** I gave each of 694 Holocene bison specimens (body-size data from Martin et al. 2018) a local temperature from this reconstruction at its coordinates and age, then compared it with two other temperature estimates (the Greenland GISP2 series and a free-running TraCE paleo-bioclim field) and with a simple time trend, using linear mixed models of body mass with a locality random effect (`mass ~ z(temperature) + (1 | locality)`).

**Headline result.** Of the three temperature estimates, the data-assimilation field predicts body size best, ahead of the single Greenland series (R²m 0.12 vs 0.04) and the free-running model (≈ 0). Even so, none of them explains body size beyond a Holocene time trend. There is no spatial Bergmann cline (latitude–mass correlation ≈ 0), and the local temperature where a bison lived adds essentially nothing once age is in the model. The thermal signal is detectable but small: Holocene bison body size tracks time more closely than temperature. This points away from a purely thermal mechanism and toward the non-thermal hypotheses (forage productivity and quality, density, and their interaction; cf. Hill et al. 2008), which this reconstruction does not yet test. Provisional paleo-precipitation added no detectable body-size signal (likelihood-ratio tests n.s.); whether effective moisture and productivity drive the trend is an open question, best addressed with observation-constrained hydroclimate.

**Design caveat.** The dataset is effectively cross-sectional: each of the 37 localities has a single age, and the reconstruction is nearly constant within a locality. Age and local temperature are therefore strongly collinear (specimen-level r ≈ −0.89), and the comparison contrasts time-specific localities more than it tests temperature change within a site (effective sample size ≈ 37 localities). The main conclusion holds despite this: adding local temperature to a time trend does not improve fit (likelihood-ratio test p = 0.53), and the temperature coefficient flips sign once age is included. But the analysis can only bound the thermal signal, not isolate it; a richer set of multi-aged localities, or within-site time series, would sharpen the test.

**Notes.** Species (*antiquus / occidentalis / bison*) were excluded as a predictor: they form an anagenetic continuum and body size is itself a species-diagnostic character, so conditioning on species would be circular. Temp12k proxy coverage thins south of ~32°N, so the warm southern end of the bison range is more prior-driven.

## Method

This reconstruction reproduces the offline paleoclimate data-assimilation method of [Erb et al. (2022)](https://doi.org/10.5194/cp-18-2599-2022). The prior is constructed from transient climate model simulations (HadCM3 and TraCE-21ka), regridded and time-averaged to the user-specified resolution. Proxies are sourced from [LiPDverse](https://lipdverse.org). The original reconstruction code is available at [Holocene-Reconstruction/Holocene-code](https://github.com/Holocene-Reconstruction/Holocene-code).
