## About this reconstruction — a bison body-size case study

North American bison underwent a pronounced reduction in body size through the Late Quaternary, and the cause has been debated for decades. Several hypotheses compete to explain it: thermal responses to a warming climate (Bergmann's rule), declining forage productivity and quality, density-dependent effects of population change, and human hunting pressure — likely acting together and episodically (e.g. Hill et al. 2008). This custom Holocene reconstruction, generated for the **PReSto Workshop (June 2026)**, isolates one strand of that debate — the **thermal** hypothesis — and tests it by representing temperature as accurately as possible at the place and time each animal lived.

**Why a better temperature field matters.** Thermal tests have usually leaned on a single hemispheric temperature series. Martin et al. (2018, *Ecology & Evolution*), whose body-size dataset I reuse here, related bison body mass to a **Greenland ice-core (GISP2)** record. A single global series like this varies only in time, so on its own it cannot separate a temperature effect from the secular passage of time, and it carries no information about the local climate each bison actually experienced. Observation-constrained, spatially-explicit data assimilation removes both limitations, letting the thermal hypothesis be evaluated on its strongest footing rather than on a single proxy.

**What this reconstruction is.** An observation-constrained, spatially-explicit **annual-mean surface-temperature** reconstruction for North America over **0–12,000 cal BP**, produced by Holocene paleoclimate **data assimilation** (Temp12k proxies assimilated into a HadCM3 + TraCE-21ka model prior; 100-yr steps; 104-member ensemble). Full settings are in the *Reconstruction parameters* and *Climate proxies* sections below.

**How I used it.** I assigned each of **694 Holocene bison specimens** (body-size data from Martin et al. 2018) a local temperature sampled from this reconstruction at its coordinates and age, and compared it against two other temperature representations — the Greenland GISP2 series and a free-running TraCE paleo-bioclim field — and against a simple time trend, using linear mixed models of body mass with a locality random effect (`mass ~ z(temperature) + (1 | locality)`).

**Headline result.** Among the three temperature representations, the observation-constrained data-assimilation field is the most informative predictor of body size — more so than the single Greenland series (R²m 0.12 vs 0.04) or the free-running model (≈ 0). Yet **no temperature formulation explains body size beyond a secular Holocene trend**: there is no spatial Bergmann cline (latitude–mass correlation ≈ 0), and the local DA temperature where a bison lived adds essentially nothing once time is accounted for. A well-resolved thermal signal is therefore detectable but small — Holocene bison body size tracks **time** more than **temperature**. That narrows the field rather than settling it: it points away from a purely thermal mechanism and toward the non-thermal hypotheses — forage productivity and quality, density, and their interaction (cf. Hill et al. 2008) — which this reconstruction does not yet test. Provisional paleo-precipitation added no detectable body-size signal (likelihood-ratio tests n.s.); whether effective moisture and productivity drive the trend remains an open question, best addressed with observation-constrained hydroclimate.

**Design caveat.** The dataset is effectively cross-sectional: each of the 37 localities carries a single age, and the reconstruction is near-constant within a locality, so age and local temperature are strongly collinear (specimen-level r ≈ −0.89) and the comparison reflects differences *between* time-specific localities rather than a controlled *within*-site temporal test (effective sample size ≈ 37 localities). The conclusion is robust to this — adding local temperature to a simple time trend does not improve fit (likelihood-ratio test p = 0.53), and the temperature coefficient even reverses sign once time is included — but it means the analysis *bounds* the thermal signal rather than delivering a temporal experiment. A richer set of multi-aged localities, or within-site time series, would sharpen the test.

**Notes.** Species (*antiquus / occidentalis / bison*) were deliberately **excluded** as a predictor: they form an anagenetic continuum and body size is itself a species-diagnostic character, so conditioning on species is circular. Temp12k proxy coverage thins south of ~32°N, so the warm/southern end of the bison range is more prior-driven.
